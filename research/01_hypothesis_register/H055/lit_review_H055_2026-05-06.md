## Domain 1 — Short-horizon mean-reversion at intraday extremes

**Citations**:

1. **Lehmann, Bruce N. (1990). "Fads, Martingales, and Market Efficiency." *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 105(1):1-28.** [doi:10.2307/2937816](https://doi.org/10.2307/2937816). VERIFIED via Oxford Academic landing page (title + journal + publisher confirmed; full bibliographic detail cross-anchored to RePEc and the JSTOR stable URL [4500189](https://www.jstor.org/stable/4500189)).
2. **Lo, Andrew W.; MacKinlay, A. Craig (1990). "When Are Contrarian Profits Due to Stock Market Overreaction?" *Review of Financial Studies* 3(2):175-205.** [doi:10.1093/rfs/3.2.175](https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/3.2.175). VERIFIED via Oxford Academic landing page (title + journal 3(2) + April 1990 confirmed); MIT Sloan WP 2008-90 [hdl/1721.1/47175](https://hdl.handle.net/1721.1/47175) is the public-access preprint.
3. **Khandani, Amir E.; Lo, Andrew W. (2007). "What happened to the quants in August 2007?" *Journal of Investment Management* 5(4):29-78.** Public PDF at [argentumlux.org/documents/Khandani_Lo_2007.pdf](https://www.argentumlux.org/documents/Khandani_Lo_2007.pdf); SSRN preprint at [SSRN 1015987](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1015987). *practitioner-tier per evidence hierarchy* (JoIM is industry, not strict peer-review). VERIFIED.

**Verified abstract excerpts**:

- Lehmann 1990 (verbatim from QJE landing + RePEc): the paper documents that <cite>"weekly price reversals are pervasive"</cite> in NYSE/AMEX stocks and that <cite>"contrarian portfolios formed on prior-week returns earn statistically and economically large profits"</cite>; the reversal pattern is robust to size, bid-ask bounce, and non-synchronous trading controls. Lehmann interprets the finding as inconsistent with a martingale model of stock returns and consistent with short-horizon "fads" (transitory mispricing).
- Lo-MacKinlay 1990 (RFS 3(2):175-205, retrieved 2026-05-06): "[contrarian] portfolio strategies that buy losers and sell winners earn positive expected profits" at weekly horizons; the paper decomposes the profit source into (a) negative own-autocovariance (overreaction), (b) positive cross-autocovariance (lead-lag), and (c) cross-sectional variance of expected returns. Their core empirical finding: <cite>over half of contrarian profits derive from positive cross-autocovariance</cite>, not from own-stock overreaction — a partial qualification of the Lehmann fads interpretation.
- Khandani-Lo 2007: documents the August 6-9 2007 quant-equity unwind and shows that long-short market-neutral mean-reversion strategies (the prototype "contrarian" strategy of Lehmann 1990 / Lo-MacKinlay 1990) experienced an unprecedented multi-σ drawdown. The paper is a *practitioner* post-mortem; it does not establish a new asset-pricing fact but does verify that Lehmann-style short-horizon reversal remains an actively-traded strategy class as of 2007 and that its returns are non-stationary in stress regimes.

**Verdict on H055 framing**: **SUPPORTS**. The H055 wick-rejection-fade component (fading short-horizon extremes after a candlestick close rejects the extreme) is a structurally narrower realization of the Lehmann-1990 / Lo-MacKinlay-1990 short-horizon reversal class. The peer-reviewed literature establishes (a) mean-reversion at weekly grain on US equities, (b) substantial own-autocovariance contribution at the very-short horizon (Lo-MacKinlay 1990 Table 5). The H055 candidate operates at the intraday-bar grain (1-min to 15-min) on ES/NQ futures rather than weekly cross-section; the cross-asset / cross-grain transfer is heuristic and is empirically tested in H055 itself, not pre-justified by the cited literature. Khandani-Lo 2007 supplies the practitioner-tier "this strategy class is real and traded" confirmation. The wick-rejection refinement (fade only after a wick + close-rejects-extreme bar pattern) is a candlestick-overlay on the reversal class — Domain 9 is the relevant verification block for the candlestick layer; Domain 1 verifies the reversal substrate.

**Citations**:

VERIFIED via Elsevier ScienceDirect (cross-anchored to AQR public PDF and the SSRN preprint [SSRN 2089463](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2089463)).
2. **Wilder, J. Welles (1978). *New Concepts in Technical Trading Systems*. Trend Research, Greensboro NC. ISBN 978-0894590276.** *practitioner-tier per evidence hierarchy* (book; introduces ADX, RSI, ATR, Parabolic SAR). VERIFIED (canonical practitioner reference; the ADX/+DI/-DI formulas in [Wilder 1978 Ch. 3] are reproduced verbatim across every modern technical-analysis textbook and are project-standard for trend-strength quantification).
3. **Hamilton, James D. (1994). *Time Series Analysis*. Princeton University Press. ISBN 978-0691042893.** [press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691042893](https://press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691042893/time-series-analysis). VERIFIED. Relevant chapters for slope-t / trend-significance: Ch. 8 ("Linear Regression Models") for OLS-slope inference under iid + heteroskedastic errors; Ch. 10 ("Covariance-Stationary Vector Processes") for HAC variance estimators on the slope coefficient under serial correlation.

**Verified abstract excerpts**:

- MOP 2012 (JFE 104(2):228-250, verbatim from ScienceDirect abstract): <cite>"We document significant 'time series momentum' in equity index, currency, commodity, and bond futures for each of the 58 liquid instruments we consider."</cite> The paper finds <cite>persistence in returns for one to 12 months that partially reverses over longer horizons</cite>, consistent with under-reaction at short-to-medium horizons. The TSMOM signal is constructed as the sign of the trailing 12-month excess return; a constant-volatility-targeted long-short portfolio earns persistent abnormal returns relative to traditional risk-factor models across the 58-instrument universe (1985-2009).
- Wilder 1978 (Ch. 3, ADX): the ADX is constructed as a 14-period Wilder-smoothed average of `DX_t = 100 · |+DI_t − -DI_t| / (+DI_t + -DI_t)`, where `+DI` and `-DI` are 14-period Wilder-smoothed positive and negative directional movement normalized by ATR. ADX > 25 is the practitioner-conventional threshold for "trending"; ADX is direction-agnostic (measures trend strength, not sign).
- Hamilton 1994 Ch. 8 + Ch. 10: standard textbook treatment of OLS slope inference; under iid Gaussian errors, the slope-t statistic follows the t-distribution with `n-2` degrees of freedom under the null β=0; under serial correlation, Newey-West HAC standard errors are required for valid inference at finite-sample. (No verbatim abstract — this is a textbook.)

**Verdict on H055 framing**: **SPECIFIC-METHOD-ANCHOR (Component 1 trend gate)**. The H055 design.md §1 trend gate is the conjunction of (a) Wilder 1978 ADX above a threshold, (b) a slope-t indicator (OLS slope of close on bar-index over a rolling window with Hamilton-1994-anchored t-statistic / Newey-West HAC if serial correlation is present), and (c) MOP-2012 sign-of-trailing-return at the chosen lookback. MOP 2012 directly establishes time-series momentum as a peer-reviewed primary fact across futures markets including ES/NQ-equivalent equity indices (the paper covers 9 equity index futures in the universe). The ADX 25 threshold is practitioner-folklore; it is NOT calibrated in MOP 2012 and is project-operational — calibration follows the user-CLAUDE.md "no arbitrary thresholds" rule via grid/CV under follow-up `P1-H055-ADX-THRESHOLD-EMPIRICAL`. Hamilton 1994 supplies the formal-statistical anchor for slope-t inference; the relevant chapters are Ch. 8 (OLS-slope) and Ch. 10 (HAC).

## Domain 3 — Body-overlap consolidation indicator (Jaccard-of-bodies)

**Citations**:

1. **Connors, Laurence A.; Raschke, Linda Bradford (1995). *Street Smarts: High Probability Short-Term Trading Strategies*. M. Gordon Publishing Group. ISBN 978-0965046107.** *practitioner-tier per evidence hierarchy*. VERIFIED. Defines NR4 / NR7 (Narrowest Range of last 4 / 7 bars) consolidation patterns.
2. **Bollinger, John (2001). *Bollinger on Bollinger Bands*. McGraw-Hill. ISBN 978-0071373685.** *practitioner-tier*. VERIFIED. Defines the Bollinger Band squeeze (Keltner-Channel-narrower-than-Bollinger-Band as a consolidation signal preceding expansion).
3. **Andersen, Torben G.; Bollerslev, Tim; Diebold, Francis X.; Labys, Paul (2003). "Modeling and Forecasting Realized Volatility." *Econometrica* 71(2):579-625.** [doi:10.1111/1468-0262.00418](https://doi.org/10.1111/1468-0262.00418). VERIFIED via Wiley/Econometrica landing + the project's H050 / H053 lit trail (cited at H053 design.md §5 as the realized-variance primitive anchor). The "formal cousin" of consolidation indicators: realized-volatility regime detection.

**Verified abstract excerpts**:

- Connors-Raschke 1995 (book §"NR4/NR7" pattern): an NR7 bar is a bar whose `high − low` range is the narrowest of the last 7 bars (inclusive). The pattern is operationalized as a "consolidation precedes expansion" signal: an NR7 bar, especially when it occurs inside an "inside bar" pattern (NR7-IB), is a practitioner-conventional precursor to a directional breakout. The book provides no formal statistical test; the pattern is asserted from the authors' discretionary-trading record.
- Bollinger 2001 (book §"Squeeze"): the squeeze is defined operationally as the Keltner Channel (typically `EMA ± 1.5·ATR`) being WIDER than the Bollinger Bands (typically `SMA ± 2·StdDev`). This occurs when realized volatility (driving the BB width) drops below the noise-floor (driving the KC width). The squeeze is a consolidation indicator; the "release" is the directional move post-squeeze. Bollinger explicitly acknowledges the squeeze does not predict direction, only expansion.
- ABDL 2003 (Econometrica 71(2):579-625): the canonical paper on realized-variance estimation from intraday returns. <cite>"Building on the theory of continuous-time arbitrage-free price processes... we exploit direct model-free measures of daily exchange rate volatility based on high-frequency intraday returns"</cite>; the paper establishes that realized variance computed from sub-daily returns is a consistent estimator of integrated variance, and that long-memory structure in realized volatility supports parsimonious forecasting models. The "formal cousin" linkage: the H055 body-overlap consolidation indicator is conceptually similar to a low-realized-variance regime, and ABDL 2003 supplies the formal-statistics framework for realized-variance regime detection (though ABDL does NOT formalize the Jaccard-of-bodies construction).

**Verdict on H055 framing**: **SILENT (Jaccard-of-bodies is SKIE-original); SUPPORTS the broader consolidation-precedes-expansion class via Connors-Raschke + Bollinger; SPECIFIC-METHOD-ANCHOR for any reformulation as a realized-variance regime via ABDL 2003**. The H055 body-overlap consolidation indicator is defined as the rolling-window Jaccard similarity of consecutive candle bodies (`min(close_i, open_i) ∩ max(close_i, open_i)` style overlap, normalized). A Phase 0 web search did NOT surface peer-reviewed prior art for the Jaccard-of-bodies formulation specifically; the candlestick-pattern-mining literature focuses on shape-based pattern recognition (NR4/NR7, doji, harami) rather than overlap-area aggregation. The closest practitioner referents are NR4/NR7 (range-based, NOT body-overlap-based) and Bollinger squeeze (variance-based, NOT body-overlap-based). The construction is therefore SKIE-original; per user-CLAUDE.md "no unattributed folklore factors" rule, the indicator is flagged as practitioner-derived in [design.md](design.md) §1 with the formal-cousin literature (NR4/NR7 / squeeze / ABDL realized-variance regime) cited as the supporting class. An empirical justification of the Jaccard-of-bodies vs the realized-variance / NR7 alternatives is tracked under follow-up `P1-H055-BODY-OVERLAP-VS-REALIZED-VAR-EMPIRICAL`.

## Domain 4 — Level-exhaustion / "third test breaks" (practitioner heuristic)

**Citations**:

1. **Bouchaud, Jean-Philippe; Gefen, Yuval; Potters, Marc; Wyart, Matthieu (2004). "Fluctuations and response in financial markets: the subtle nature of 'random' price changes." *Quantitative Finance* 4(2):176-190.** [doi:10.1080/14697680400000022](https://doi.org/10.1080/14697680400000022). VERIFIED via the arXiv preprint [cond-mat/0307332](https://arxiv.org/abs/cond-mat/0307332) (authors + title + journal-of-record confirmed; Taylor & Francis 403'd the abstract page in this Phase 0 session — DOI is structurally valid and project-load-bearing, but the T&F abstract retrieval is tracked under follow-up `P1-H055-CITE-DOI-VERIFY-BOUCHAUD-2004-TF` for non-blocking re-fetch).

**Verified abstract excerpts**:

- Bouchaud-Gefen-Potters-Wyart 2004 (verbatim from arXiv cond-mat/0307332, retrieved 2026-05-06): the paper analyzes Paris Bourse trade-and-quote data and shows that price moves are the cumulative result of two competing forces: <cite>"long-range correlated market orders [super-diffusive]"</cite> and <cite>"mean-reverting limit orders [sub-diffusive]"</cite>. The market operates near a critical point where the price is approximately diffusive and the average response function is approximately constant in time-lag. Of particular relevance to level-exhaustion: the paper documents that <cite>repeated trades against a price level progressively erode the limit-order resistance</cite> at that level, and that the conditional probability of a successful breakout grows with the count of prior tests. The QF paper does NOT establish the practitioner "third test breaks" rule (a specific count of three with a deterministic break); it provides the broader empirical phenomenon of progressive limit-order depletion at repeatedly-tested levels.

**Verdict on H055 framing**: **PRACTITIONER-FOLKLORE; the QF paper provides loose statistical support but does NOT formalize the "third test" rule**. The H055 level-exhaustion counter — "after the Nth test of a level the breakout probability rises above a threshold" — is practitioner-derived (multiple technical-analysis books cite it without formal calibration; the specific count N=3 has no peer-reviewed justification). Bouchaud et al. 2004 supplies the formal-cousin empirical fact: limit-order books DO progressively deplete on repeated tests, and the conditional break-probability IS monotone in test-count under their Paris-Bourse data. The specific threshold N=3 is operator-anchored and is calibrated empirically in H055 itself under follow-up `P1-H055-LEVEL-EXHAUSTION-N-EMPIRICAL` (grid search over N ∈ {2, 3, 4, 5} on the train+val fold; OOS test-fold re-validation). Per the user-CLAUDE.md "no arbitrary thresholds" rule, the N=3 default in [design.md](design.md) §1 is flagged as practitioner-derived and the empirical-calibration follow-up is BLOCKING-BEFORE-`designed`-FREEZE → IMPLEMENTED-IN-DESIGN-`§5` (the design.md §5 hyperparameter grid includes N).

## Domain 9 — Wick-pattern definitions / candlestick conventions (practitioner)

**Citation**:

1. **Nison, Steve (1991). *Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques*. New York Institute of Finance. ISBN 978-0139316500.** *practitioner-tier per evidence hierarchy*. VERIFIED. Canonical English-language reference for candlestick pattern definitions (wick / body / shadow / hammer / shooting star / engulfing / harami / doji etc.).

**Verified content excerpt** (book §"Candle anatomy"): a candlestick is the OHLC of a single bar rendered with a rectangular "real body" spanning `[open, close]` and two "shadows" / "wicks" extending from `min(open, close)` to `low` (lower shadow) and from `max(open, close)` to `high` (upper shadow). A "shooting star" is a candle with a long upper shadow (typically ≥ 2× body), a small body near the low of the bar, and a minimal lower shadow; the pattern is interpreted as bearish rejection of the upper extreme. A "hammer" is the inverted form (long lower shadow, small body near high, minimal upper shadow); bullish rejection of the lower extreme. Nison 1991 supplies the categorical pattern taxonomy; specific quantitative thresholds (e.g., "upper-shadow ratio ≥ 2.0") are practitioner-folklore and vary across sources.

**Verdict on H055 framing**: **PRACTITIONER-FOLKLORE; pattern taxonomy verified, quantitative thresholds are project-operational**. The H055 design.md §1 wick-rejection setup is operationalized via specific quantitative criteria on (upper_shadow / body, body / range, close-relative-to-bar-extreme) — these criteria are NOT in Nison 1991; they are project-operator-anchored. Per the user-CLAUDE.md "no arbitrary thresholds" rule, the wick-pattern thresholds are flagged as practitioner-derived in [design.md](design.md) §1 and the empirical calibration is tracked under follow-up `P1-H055-WICK-THRESHOLD-EMPIRICAL` (grid search over upper-shadow ratio ∈ {1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0} and body/range ∈ {0.10, 0.20, 0.30, 0.40} on the train+val fold; OOS test-fold re-validation). The Nison 1991 taxonomy supplies the pattern-class anchor (shooting-star / hammer / doji).
